RF Replay Attack — Brief Explanation

TL;DR

An RF replay attack captures a legitimate radio-frequency (RF) transmission (for example, from a remote key fob or wireless sensor) and later retransmits that same recorded signal to impersonate the original device. It can give an attacker temporary control (e.g., unlocking a car or gate) when devices accept a repeated signal as valid.

What it is (plain terms)

An RF replay attack is a type of wireless impersonation where an attacker:

  1. Listens to a wireless transmission between a legitimate transmitter (like a key fob) and its receiver.
  2. Records the transmitted radio signal.
  3. Replays that recorded signal back to the receiver at a later time to reproduce the original action.

Because the receiver sees the same signal it received before, it may accept it and perform the associated function.

Common targets / examples (high level)

  • Car remote key fobs (unlock/lock, alarm)
  • Garage door and gate remotes
  • Simple wireless doorbells and smart locks with poor radio protocols
  • Wireless sensor triggers (some alarm systems)

Note: Modern secure systems often use countermeasures (see below); older or simpler devices are most at risk.

How receivers are tricked (high level)

Some systems accept any correctly formatted RF transmission that matches an expected pattern. If the device does not ensure the signal is fresh or uniquely tied to a single event (e.g., by using rolling codes, timestamps, or cryptographic verification), then a previously captured message will still be accepted when replayed.

Defenses & mitigations (what manufacturers and users should do)

  • Rolling codes / rolling nonces: Each button press generates a new code that the receiver will accept only once. Replaying an old code is rejected.
  • Cryptographic authentication: Use secure, modern crypto (challenge–response, symmetric/asymmetric auth) rather than fixed or predictable codes.
  • Time-limited tokens: Messages include timestamps or short-lived tokens the receiver validates.
  • Signal validation and telemetry: Verify additional metadata (message sequence numbers, device state) that makes a simple capture/replay invalid.
  • Physical precautions: Keep remotes secure; avoid leaving them near entry points; be cautious in public places.
  • Firmware / product updates: Manufacturers should patch insecure protocols and provide updates for devices that can be upgraded.
  • Use proven standards: Choose products that document secure RF protocols and third-party security reviews.

Detection (high level)

  • Repeated unexpected activations or unlocking events.
  • Logs showing duplicate command values or sequence anomalies.
  • Users noticing devices activating with no button press.

Legal & ethical note

Recording or replaying RF signals you do not own or have explicit permission to test can be illegal. If you discover a vulnerability, follow responsible disclosure: contact the vendor or owner and avoid exploiting the issue.

Bottom line

An RF replay attack is simple in concept (record + replay) but only works against devices that do not protect against reuse of old messages. Modern safety comes from rolling codes, cryptographic challenge–response, and careful product design.
